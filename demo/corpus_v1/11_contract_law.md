# Contract Formation and Enforcement

Contract law governs the creation and enforcement of legally binding agreements between parties. Understanding the essential elements of a valid contract and the remedies available for breach is fundamental to commercial transactions and business relationships.

## Essential Elements of a Valid Contract

### Offer and Acceptance

A contract begins with a definite offer by one party and an unequivocal acceptance by another. The offer must be sufficiently specific in its terms, including identification of the parties, subject matter, price or consideration, and time for performance. An invitation to treat, such as a product displayed on a store shelf, is distinguished from a binding offer because it merely invites the other party to make an offer.

Acceptance must mirror the terms of the offer under the common law mirror image rule. Any material alteration constitutes a counteroffer rather than an acceptance. Under the Uniform Commercial Code, which governs the sale of goods, additional terms in an acceptance may become part of the contract between merchants unless the offer expressly limits acceptance to its terms, the additional terms materially alter the agreement, or the offeror objects within a reasonable time.

### Consideration

Consideration requires that each party provide something of value or assume an obligation they were not previously bound to perform. Past consideration is generally insufficient, as it represents a benefit already conferred without expectation of reciprocal obligation. Adequacy of consideration is typically not examined by courts, meaning that parties are free to make bargains that may appear unequal in value.

### Capacity and Legality

Both parties must have the legal capacity to enter into a contract. Minors, individuals adjudicated as mentally incapacitated, and persons under the influence of substances may lack capacity. The subject matter of the contract must be legal; agreements to perform illegal acts are void and unenforceable.

## Defenses to Enforcement

Several doctrines may render an otherwise valid contract unenforceable. Duress involves coercion that overcomes a party's free will. Undue influence arises when a party in a position of trust exploits that relationship. Unconscionability applies when contract terms are so one-sided that no reasonable person would agree to them, and the disadvantaged party had no meaningful choice.

The statute of frauds requires certain categories of contracts to be evidenced by a signed writing, including contracts for the sale of land, agreements that cannot be performed within one year, and contracts for the sale of goods exceeding $500 under the UCC.

## Remedies for Breach

The non-breaching party may pursue several remedies. Compensatory damages restore the injured party to the position they would have occupied had the contract been performed. Consequential damages cover foreseeable losses resulting from the breach, such as lost profits. Specific performance, an equitable remedy, compels the breaching party to perform their obligation and is typically available only when monetary damages are inadequate, such as in contracts involving unique real property.

Liquidated damages clauses, agreed upon at the time of contracting, specify the amount of damages payable upon breach. Courts enforce these clauses when the stipulated amount is a reasonable estimate of anticipated harm and when actual damages would be difficult to calculate.
